Redesigning my portfolio, with AI as a collaborator

Portfolio Redesign —
An AI-Assisted Design Process

Overview

My portfolio is my most important product — it's the one screen a recruiter judges before they ever meet me. The old version showed nice screens but told weak stories, mixed three visual styles, and made hiring managers work to understand my impact.

This is the end-to-end case study of how I rebuilt it: a full audit, a single design system, rewritten case-study narratives, and a hand-coded, deployed site — using AI as a research partner, writing editor and critique loop, while keeping every design decision my own.

01 — Project Overview

Treating my portfolio as a product, not a gallery

The goal wasn't a prettier site. It was to shorten the distance between "recruiter opens the link" and "recruiter understands what I can do for their team."

I decided to redesign because I was applying for Senior Product Designer roles in Berlin and noticed a pattern: strong conversations happened when someone had actually read a case study, and weak ones when they'd only skimmed screens. My old portfolio optimised for the skim. It showed outcomes but hid the thinking — the exact thing a senior hiring bar is testing for.

Target audience. Three readers, each skimming differently: recruiters (30-second scan for role fit, seniority, location), hiring managers (5-minute read for decision-making and craft), and design teams on the panel (deep read for process rigour and systems thinking). Every layout choice was checked against those three reading speeds.

Role & responsibilities. This was a solo, full-stack design project — I owned research, information architecture, UX writing, visual design, the design system, front-end build, and deployment. There was no engineer to hand off to and no PM to write the brief, so I acted as all three.

02 — Initial Audit & Problem Identification

Finding out why the old portfolio wasn't converting

I audited the existing site the same way I'd audit a client product: a heuristic pass against usability and communication principles, a read of my own analytics and recruiter replies, and a benchmark against portfolios that were clearly landing interviews. I wrote every problem as a specific, fixable statement rather than a vague "it feels off."

Problem 01
Weak storytelling
Projects were screen dumps. No problem statement, no research, no decisions — so a reader couldn't tell what I actually did versus what the team did. Impact was implied, never shown.
Problem 02
Three visual styles fighting
Each project page had its own colours, type and spacing. The site read like three different designers. No shared tokens meant no consistency — and inconsistency reads as junior.
Problem 03
Typography & hierarchy noise
Too many weights and sizes with no scale. Headlines and body blurred together, so the eye had no path. Nothing signalled "read this first."
Problem 04
Colour imbalance
Accent colours were used decoratively, not functionally. Nothing tied to meaning, and several text/background pairings failed contrast — an accessibility and credibility problem at once.
Problem 05
Generic templated layouts
The old site looked like a theme. Nothing about it felt authored — and for a design role, the portfolio itself is the strongest craft sample I have.
Problem 06
Usability friction
No clear way back to Work, no obvious next project, buried resume link, and a mobile layout that broke on the pages recruiters open most from LinkedIn.

Competitor & inspiration research

I studied portfolios and reference sites that consistently earn senior interviews, then extracted the pattern behind each — not the pixels. The takeaway was consistent: strong portfolios lead with a decision and a result, use one restrained system, and make the reading path effortless.

"The best portfolios don't show more work — they make one project impossible to misunderstand."

— The principle I kept returning to during the audit
03 — AI-Assisted Design Process

Where AI helped — and where I stayed in charge

I used AI (Claude and ChatGPT) as a fast, tireless collaborator across the process — a research assistant, a writing editor, and an on-demand critique partner. It compressed the slow parts of the work so I could spend my time on judgement. Crucially, AI never made a design decision; it widened my options and pressure-tested mine.

Research assistance

Faster synthesis of what "good" looks like

I asked AI to summarise recurring patterns in senior product-design portfolios and to translate WCAG contrast rules into a plain checklist I could design against.

My decisionI chose which patterns fit a systems-and-research designer and discarded the flashy ones that didn't match my strengths.
Idea & reference generation

Divergent options, quickly

I generated multiple directions for hero layouts, palette moods and section structures to react against — using AI to get past the blank canvas.

My decisionI converged on a calm dark-green editorial direction with a single teal accent, because it reads senior and doesn't compete with the work.
UX writing & storytelling

Sharper case-study narratives

AI helped me tighten problem statements, cut filler, and turn feature lists into decision-and-result sentences. I fed it my raw notes; it gave me leaner drafts.

My decisionEvery final line is in my voice — I rewrote anything that sounded generic or overclaimed, and kept the specifics only I know.
Content structure

A repeatable case-study skeleton

Together we defined one structure — Problem → Research → Insight → Process → Solution → Impact → Learnings — so all three case studies scan the same way.

My decisionI set the structure as a rule and enforced it in the actual component system, not just the copy.
Design critique

A second set of eyes, on demand

I pasted screenshots and described choices, then asked AI to argue the recruiter's, the manager's, and the panellist's point of view separately.

My decisionI treated critiques as hypotheses to test against real users — not instructions to follow.
Iteration speed

Tighter feedback loops

Because drafting, rewriting and debugging were faster, I ran more cycles in the same time — more versions of the hero, the work list, the case-study intro.

My decisionMore iterations only helped because I set the bar for what counted as "better" each round.
04 — Design System & Visual Improvements

One system, applied everywhere

The single biggest upgrade was replacing three ad-hoc styles with one tokenised system, defined once and shared across every page through a single shared.css file. Change a token, and the whole site updates — the same discipline I bring to product design systems.

Colour palette & accessibility

I built a restrained palette around a deep forest green as the brand foundation, a single teal as the functional accent (links, labels, active states), and a warm yellow reserved only for availability and highlights. Every pairing was checked for WCAG AA contrast: body text sits at 4.5:1 or higher, and colour never carries meaning alone — it's always paired with text or shape.

Typography & hierarchy

I paired a serif for voice with a sans for clarity and a mono for structural labels — a three-role system, not a pile of fonts. Newsreader (italic, light) carries editorial headlines and lead lines; Source Sans handles all body and UI; Space Mono is reserved for eyebrow labels, meta and numbers. A fixed type scale replaced the old free-for-all, so hierarchy now guides the eye instead of fighting it.

Spacing, components & consistency

I standardised spacing with a fluid clamp() padding token and a shared max-width, so rhythm is identical on every page and every breakpoint. Recurring patterns became named components in shared.css — insight cards, process steps, personas, outcome metrics, quote blocks, before/after callouts and case navigation. Because each one is defined once, the whole portfolio stays visually coherent and is trivial to extend with a new project.

Motion & micro-interactions

Motion is used sparingly and with intent. A custom blend-mode cursor that grows over interactive targets signals interactivity; content reveals on scroll via an IntersectionObserver so sections arrive with a calm rhythm; the work list shifts and reveals meta on hover to invite exploration; and a marquee of past brands adds life without noise. Every animation respects reduced-motion and never blocks reading.

05 — Information Architecture

Reorganising for how recruiters actually read

I restructured the site around the recruiter's journey rather than my own chronology. The landing page now leads with a one-line positioning statement, availability and location (the three things a recruiter checks first), then a scannable Selected Work list, a "What I do" grid for capability, and social proof via past brands. Each project links to a case study that always ends with a clear path to the next one.

Structural change

The old IA buried the decision-useful information. The new IA surfaces it in the first screen and keeps every case study one predictable structure.

07 — AI Prompt Engineering

How I directed AI to get useful output

Good AI output came from good briefs. I prompted AI the way I'd brief a collaborator: role, context, constraints, and the exact form of the answer I wanted. Vague prompts gave generic results; specific ones gave material I could actually design with. A few representative examples:

Redesign concept directionDivergent ideas
You are a senior design director. My portfolio targets Senior Product Designer roles in Berlin. My strengths are UX research and design systems, not flashy visuals. Suggest 5 distinct art directions for a calm, editorial, senior-feeling portfolio. For each: mood, palette logic, type pairing, and the risk. No generic "modern and clean."
Why it worked: role + audience + constraints + "the risk" forced trade-off thinking, not a list of buzzwords.
Case-study copy improvementUX writing
Here are my raw notes on the JioBP project. Rewrite the problem statement in 2 sentences that a hiring manager could repeat back after one read. Keep my specifics (fuel booking, station discovery). Cut adjectives. Then flag any sentence that claims impact I haven't backed with evidence.
Why it worked: constrained length, preserved specifics, and asked AI to police overclaiming — which I then verified.
Multi-perspective critiqueDesign review
Critique this case-study intro from three separate viewpoints: (1) a recruiter scanning for 30 seconds, (2) a hiring manager deciding whether to read on, (3) a senior designer judging craft. For each, give the single biggest reason they'd stop reading.
Why it worked: separating audiences surfaced conflicts I could resolve deliberately instead of averaging them away.
Accessibility reviewUX / a11y
Given these foreground/background hex pairs, tell me which fail WCAG AA for body text and AA-large for headings, and suggest the smallest lightness change to pass without shifting the hue.
Why it worked: a precise, checkable question — I confirmed each result in a contrast checker before shipping.
08 — Development & Implementation

Designing in the browser, then shipping it

Rather than hand off static mockups, I built the site myself in semantic HTML, CSS and vanilla JavaScript — no framework, no build step, so it stays fast and maintainable. Designing in code let me tune real type rendering, motion and responsiveness directly instead of approximating them in a mockup. AI acted as a pair-programmer for CSS edge cases and debugging, but I owned the architecture.

Front-end

Lightweight by design

Hand-written HTML/CSS/JS with a single shared stylesheet as the source of truth. No dependencies to break, instant loads, easy to extend.

Responsive

Mobile-first, fluid

Fluid type and spacing via clamp(), CSS grid/flex layouts, and breakpoints tuned for the phones recruiters open from LinkedIn.

Performance

Fast and quiet

Preconnected fonts, optimised images, GPU-friendly transforms, and reveal animations that never block the first paint.

Deploy

Versioned & hosted

Git version control, deployed on GitHub Pages at poojakurade.github.io — every change is tracked and instantly live.

09 — Testing & Iteration

Reviewing, then changing what didn't work

I reviewed the site across real devices and browsers, shared it with designer peers and a recruiter contact, and ran AI critiques from each audience's perspective. Then I acted on the feedback — the value of testing is only in what you change afterward.

What I learned

1
AI is an accelerator, not an author. It removed friction from research, writing and debugging — but the portfolio only got better because I set the bar and made every design call myself.
2
A portfolio is a product. Treating recruiters and hiring managers as users with distinct journeys changed the IA and the writing more than any visual tweak did.
3
Constraints create seniority. One accent colour, one type system and one story spine read as more confident than variety ever did.
4
Designing in code closes the gap. Building it myself meant the shipped experience matched the intent exactly — motion, type and responsiveness included.
